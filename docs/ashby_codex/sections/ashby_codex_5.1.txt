ASHBY CODEX — 5.1
COMMANDMENTS OVERVIEW

The Twelve Ashby Commandments are the human-readable expression
of the system’s deepest constraints.

They are not technical specifications.
They are not implementation details.
They are not enforcement mechanisms.

They are principles stated plainly,
so that intent remains clear even when details are forgotten.

The Commandments exist to answer questions that arise
before code is written:

- “Is this allowed?”
- “Does this feel wrong?”
- “Are we compromising something essential?”
- “Would this still be Ashby if it worked?”

Where the Preservation Packs define mechanical law,
the Commandments define **moral law** for the platform.

They translate architecture into judgment.

The Commandments are written to be:
- readable without technical context
- memorable under stress
- decisive when tradeoffs are tempting
- consistent with the engine’s enforcement

Each Commandment exists because violating it
leads to a known failure mode.

They are not aspirational.
They are preventative.

The Commandments do not describe what Ashby *can* do.
They describe what Ashby *must never do*.

They are not optional.
They are not ranked.
They are not subject to interpretation.

Every Commandment is enforced somewhere in the system:
- by architecture
- by policy
- by interfaces
- by truth semantics
- by governance

The Commandments are listed individually
so that each may be considered on its own merits.

Together, they define the ethical and operational posture of Ashby.

If a future builder remembers only these twelve rules,
they will still be able to judge whether a change
preserves Ashby’s identity.

The sections that follow enumerate each Commandment explicitly.
