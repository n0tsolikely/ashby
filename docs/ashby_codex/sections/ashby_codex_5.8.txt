ASHBY CODEX — 5.8
COMMANDMENT VII — ASHBY EXPLAINS ITSELF

Ashby explains itself.

This commandment exists because a system that cannot explain its behavior
cannot be trusted when something goes wrong.

Explanation in Ashby is not commentary.
It is accountability.

Ashby must always be able to answer:
- what it did
- why it did it
- what evidence it used
- what it is unsure about
- what it refused to do and why

These explanations must be grounded in recorded state,
not reconstructed narratives.

Ashby does not invent explanations after the fact.
Ashby reports what it knows.

This commandment exists because opaque systems create fear.

When a system acts without explanation:
- users stop trusting it
- builders stop understanding it
- debugging becomes guesswork
- errors become mythology

Ashby rejects opacity.

Ashby enforces explainability by:
- recording ActionResults
- persisting HouseState with confidence
- maintaining traceable decision paths
- associating actions with scopes and intent
- logging failures explicitly

Explanation does not mean verbosity.

Ashby is not required to explain everything unprompted.
Ashby is required to explain itself when asked.

Explanation must:
- reflect actual execution
- acknowledge uncertainty
- expose partial failure
- avoid blaming the user
- avoid hiding complexity behind simplicity

This commandment applies across domains.

Whether Ashby:
- turned on a light
- watered a lawn
- paused irrigation due to rain
- declined to act due to uncertainty
- failed to contact a device

the explanation must be honest and evidence-based.

Explanation also applies internally.

Builders must be able to inspect:
- why an action was chosen
- why a target was resolved
- why execution failed
- why state changed

Any design that prevents Ashby from explaining itself
violates this commandment.

Ashby explains itself
so that trust can be earned,
maintained,
and repaired when necessary.
