ASHBY CODEX — 7.4
ROOMS AS HUMAN LABELS

Rooms are not scopes.

They are human-facing labels applied to physical space
to support natural language interaction.

Ashby treats rooms as aliases,
not as sources of authority.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
WHY ROOMS EXIST
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Humans organize space linguistically.

They speak in terms of:
- bedrooms
- kitchens
- offices
- garages
- living rooms

These labels are useful for communication,
but they are not sufficiently precise
to define safe automation or authority.

Rooms exist to support understanding,
not to define reality.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ROOMS VS ZONES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Zones define operational reality.
Rooms describe human intent.

A room label:
- may map to one zone
- may map to part of a zone
- may map to multiple zones

A zone:
- may contain multiple rooms
- may contain no room labels
- exists independently of human naming

This separation prevents language drift
from redefining system behavior.

Ashby never reasons about authority
based on room labels alone.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ROOM MAPPING
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Room labels are mapped explicitly.

Mapping is:
- configurable
- user-defined
- reversible
- non-authoritative

Room mappings may change
without altering zone structure.

This allows:
- renaming
- cultural variation
- personal preference
- language evolution

without architectural impact.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ROOMS AND AMBIGUITY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Rooms frequently introduce ambiguity.

Examples include:
- overlapping room concepts
- informal naming
- partial references
- inconsistent usage

Ashby treats room ambiguity
as a signal to clarify,
not as permission to guess.

Room labels may inform scope resolution,
but they never override it.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ROOMS AND EXPLANATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

When Ashby explains actions,
it may reference rooms for clarity.

Explanation may use human labels.
Execution never does.

This distinction ensures that:
- explanations remain understandable
- actions remain precise
- safety is preserved

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ABSOLUTE CONSTRAINTS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Rooms must never:
- define authority
- own state
- bypass zone boundaries
- override scope law

Any design that elevates room labels
to operational authority
violates Ashby’s world model.

Rooms exist to help humans talk to Ashby.

Zones exist to keep Ashby safe.

END OF 7.4
