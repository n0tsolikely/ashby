ASHBY CODEX — 17.5
BUSINESS DOMAIN MODULES (INVENTORY-LIKE)

A business domain module is the archetype of “recorded reality”:
it manages structured domain entities (records) inside Ashby’s governed data layer.

“Inventory-like” is representative.
This archetype covers domains where Ashby is asked to:
- store, retrieve, and update structured facts (“add milk”, “remove screws”, “how many left?”)
- maintain lists, counts, categories, and status fields
- reconcile ambiguous names into concrete entities
- preserve audit trails and deterministic behavior over time
- optionally sync those records to external systems (via adapters), without losing truth posture

Business domain modules are not hardware control.
They are stateful, governed data operations.

If simple reactive modules make Ashby feel immediate,
inventory-like modules make Ashby feel reliable.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
DEFINING TRAITS (WHAT MAKES THIS ARCHETYPE UNIQUE)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A module qualifies as inventory-like when:

1) RECORD-BASED TRUTH
The primary “world” is a set of internal records (entities).
Actions modify records, not physical devices.

2) QUERY IS A FIRST-CLASS ACTION
Users frequently ask:
- “what’s in the pantry?”
- “do we have eggs?”
- “show me everything low”
This requires truthful query semantics, not just control semantics.

3) NAMING AMBIGUITY IS COMMON
Item names are fuzzy:
- multiple “milk” entries
- “batteries” across types
- “screws” across sizes
Clarification is frequent and must be handled cleanly.

4) AUDITABILITY IS REQUIRED
Business records must be reconstructable:
- who changed what
- when
- why (from the intent)

5) SYNC MAY BE OPTIONAL AND NON-AUTHORITATIVE
Some inventory-like modules may sync to external tools (spreadsheets, ERPs, apps).
External sync is never the source of truth unless explicitly configured.
Adapters implement sync. Modules define meaning.

Inventory-like modules are “data truth modules.”
Their danger is drift, not electricity.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
THE CORE GOVERNANCE DISTINCTION: INTERNAL TRUTH VS EXTERNAL MIRRORS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Inventory-like modules often touch two realities:

A) INTERNAL CANONICAL RECORD
This is Ashby’s governed record store.
If an internal write commits successfully, that internal change may be VERIFIED
because the platform itself is the authority of that record.

B) EXTERNAL MIRROR / SYNC TARGET (OPTIONAL)
This is any outside system.
External state must never be spoken as VERIFIED unless evidence exists.

Rules:
- The module must always know which layer it is speaking about.
- A successful internal commit does not automatically mean external sync succeeded.
- Truth posture must be layered:
  - internal record truth may be VERIFIED
  - external sync status may be UNKNOWN / FAILED / LAST_KNOWN until verified

If the system collapses these, it will lie.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CANONICAL RESPONSIBILITIES OF INVENTORY-LIKE MODULES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A business domain module must:

- Define canonical domain entities and fields (vendor-neutral, stable)
- Normalize user intents into record operations deterministically
- Resolve targets (records) from names using lawful ambiguity handling (15.1–15.4)
- Enforce domain constraints (required fields, allowed ranges, invariants)
- Produce ActionRequests for mutations and for governed queries
- Return ActionResults with evidence-grade outcomes
- Maintain audit-safe change summaries (without secrets)
- Support sessions for conversational continuity (record context, active list, filters)
- Respect permissions, scope boundaries, and policy gates for high-impact changes

A business domain module must never:
- invent records to satisfy a query
- silently merge entities without confirmation
- treat external sync as authoritative without explicit configuration
- bypass Truth Gate, policy, or scope law

Its job is to make data operations truthful and reconstructable.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
REPRESENTATIVE ENTITY SHAPES (NON-EXHAUSTIVE)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Inventory-like modules manage records. These are representative shapes:

- Item (canonical identity)
  - item_id (stable internal id)
  - label (human-readable name)
  - category (optional)
  - quantity (optional numeric)
  - unit (optional; e.g., count, lbs, liters)
  - location (optional; pantry, fridge, garage)
  - attributes (optional map; size, brand, notes)
  - status (active, archived, consumed, lost)
  - created_at / updated_at
  - provenance (where it came from, if configured)

- List / Collection (optional)
  - list_id
  - name (shopping list, pantry, toolkit)
  - membership rule or explicit membership
  - visibility / permissions

Rules:
- item_id must be stable and internal (not vendor ids)
- label is not identity
- multiple items may share a label
- ambiguity is normal and must not be “resolved” by guessing

Entities are the memory of the domain.
Identity must be stable.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
REPRESENTATIVE INTENTS AND ACTIONS (INVENTORY-LIKE)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

These are examples, not a required inventory.

Common intent shapes:
- inventory.add_item(label, quantity?, unit?, location?, attributes?)
- inventory.remove_item(item_ref, quantity? | all?)
- inventory.set_quantity(item_ref, quantity, unit?)
- inventory.adjust_quantity(item_ref, delta, unit?)
- inventory.move(item_ref, location)
- inventory.search(query, filters?)
- inventory.list(location? | list_id? | filters?)
- inventory.audit(item_ref) (show history / changes, if permitted)

Rules:
- “add” must create a new record only if identity is not ambiguous
- “remove” must clarify whether “remove record” vs “decrement quantity”
- “set” must not be interpreted as “adjust” unless explicit
- queries are governed actions: they must be truthful and scope-bound

Mutations and queries both require deterministic semantics.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
AMBIGUITY HANDLING IN BUSINESS DOMAINS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Ambiguity is the default in inventory-like domains.

Common ambiguity sources:
- multiple items share the same label (“milk”)
- the user refers to an attribute (“the big one”)
- location is missing (“do we have eggs?” across multiple stores)
- unit semantics differ (“two” vs “two pounds”)

Rules:
- ambiguity triggers CLARIFY, not “best guess”
- clarification options must be bounded and scope-bound
- the module must preserve a pending clarification gate for merge-and-complete (15.3)
- a correction (“not that one”) must be interpreted within the candidate set, not the full world

Inventory domains punish guessing because records persist.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SESSIONS FOR INVENTORY-LIKE MODULES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Inventory interaction is often conversational:

- “add milk”
- “make it two”
- “actually put it on the shopping list”
- “remove the other one”
- “show me what’s low”

A business domain session may preserve (bounded, expirable):
- active collection context (which list/location we’re operating on)
- last referenced record(s)
- last query filters (e.g., location=pantry, low_stock=true)
- last mutation intent shape (add/set/adjust/remove)
- pending gate state (clarification/confirmation)

Rules:
- sessions must never create new identity
- sessions must not expand target sets invisibly
- expiry is mandatory; stale sessions cannot anchor “it”
- sessions do not upgrade truth or permission

Sessions reduce friction.
They do not authorize drift.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
HIGH-IMPACT OPERATIONS AND CONFIRMATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Business domain actions can be destructive.

High-impact examples (representative):
- delete/clear an entire list
- archive all items in a location
- bulk rename categories
- merge entities

Rules:
- bulk operations may require confirmation gates (15.5 conceptually)
- destructive operations must be explicit
- “reset everything” must be refused or clarified unless the scope defines lawful meaning
- “merge” must require explicit confirmation because it changes identity relationships

Destructive record operations are irreversible at human scale.
They must be gated.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IDEMPOTENCY AND DUPLICATE INTENT IN INVENTORY DOMAINS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Duplicates are common, but unlike device control, duplicates may be intended.

Rules:
- platform idempotency prevents accidental double-commit from transport retries
- but the module must respect that repeating a command may be intentional (“add milk” twice)

Therefore:
- idempotency is applied to execution attempts of the same turn/request
- repeated user turns are treated as new intent unless a gate/session indicates otherwise

Special case: “again”
- if a valid session indicates “again” refers to the last mutation, it may be replayed
  only if replay meaning is safe and clear
- if replay could create duplicate records unintentionally, confirm or clarify

Inventory-like modules must prevent accidental duplicates without denying intentional repetition.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FAILURE MODES (AND TRUTHFUL REPORTING)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Common failure modes include:
- permission denied (not_permitted)
- schema validation failure (invalid_parameters)
- conflict / concurrent modification (conflict)
- storage unavailable (backend_unreachable)
- external sync failure (sync_failed) (optional)

Rules:
- internal commit failure must be reported as FAILED/UNKNOWN honestly
- external sync failure must not be described as internal failure if the internal commit succeeded
- partial outcomes must be explicit (internal updated, external pending/failed)
- provide safe next steps:
  retry sync, re-run query, narrow target, ask which one, or request permission

A record system must be precise about what succeeded.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
AUDIT TRAILS ARE PART OF THE DOMAIN
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Inventory-like systems must be auditable.

Rules:
- every mutation must produce a change summary:
  - what record(s)
  - what fields changed
  - old vs new (where permitted)
  - who initiated (actor context)
  - correlation ids
- history must not leak secrets or private fields outside permission
- audit visibility is scope-governed

Audit is not optional for persistent record truth.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
REPRESENTATIVE MODULE CONTRACT (NON-BINDING SHAPE)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Representative interface (conceptual):

- can_handle(intent) → bool
- normalize(intent) → normalized_intent
- plan(normalized_intent, scope, policy, sessions, state) → plan_outcome
  where plan_outcome is:
  - ACT with ActionRequest(s) (mutations and/or governed queries), or
  - CLARIFY with pending gate, or
  - CONFIRM with pending gate, or
  - REFUSE with reason label

The engine remains the arbiter of policy outcomes.
The module provides domain structure and meaning.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ABSOLUTE CONSTRAINTS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Business domain modules (inventory-like) must:
- remain vendor-neutral and removable
- treat internal records as canonical only when configured as such
- separate internal truth from external sync truth
- enforce ambiguity handling and merge-and-complete gates
- preserve audit trails for every mutation
- use sessions only as bounded continuity, never as authority
- remain scope-bound, permission-bound, and policy-governed
- remain truthful about partial outcomes

Business domain modules must never:
- guess identity when labels are ambiguous
- invent records or quantities to satisfy queries
- merge or delete records without explicit consent where consequences are large
- treat external mirrors as authoritative without explicit configuration
- bypass Truth Gate or present sync as verification
- hide partial failures behind fluent summaries

Inventory-like modules are where Ashby becomes a trusted system of record.
