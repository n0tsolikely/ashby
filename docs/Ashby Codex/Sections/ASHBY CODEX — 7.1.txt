ASHBY CODEX — 7.1
WHAT A SCOPE IS

A scope is the fundamental unit of reality in Ashby.

It is not a label.
It is not a convenience abstraction.
It is not a user-facing grouping.

A scope is a formally bounded context that defines
what exists, what may be acted upon, and what authority applies.

If something does not exist inside a scope,
it does not exist to Ashby.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
THE PURPOSE OF SCOPES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Ashby operates in environments where:
- multiple domains coexist
- authority is not uniform
- state changes over time
- actions have consequences

Without scopes, these environments collapse into ambiguity.

Scopes exist to make boundaries explicit.

They define:
- the limits of action
- the ownership of state
- the validity of memory
- the reach of automation
- the context of reasoning

A scope answers the question:
“Where is this action allowed to occur?”

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SCOPES AS BOUNDARIES OF AUTHORITY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Every scope establishes authority.

Authority determines:
- what Ashby may control
- what Ashby may observe
- what Ashby may remember
- what Ashby may automate

Authority is never assumed.
Authority is never inferred.
Authority is always scoped.

An action that is valid in one scope
may be invalid or forbidden in another.

This prevents:
- accidental overreach
- unsafe automation
- cross-domain contamination
- silent expansion of control

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SCOPES AND EXISTENCE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Scopes define existence.

Devices, entities, state, and history
exist only within scopes.

There is no global device.
There is no global state.
There is no global memory.

Global assumptions are forbidden.

If Ashby cannot determine the scope,
it must not act.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SCOPES AND REASONING
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

All reasoning in Ashby is scoped.

Intent is resolved within a scope.
Policy is evaluated within a scope.
Execution targets are selected within a scope.
State is updated within a scope.

This ensures that reasoning remains contextual,
bounded,
and explainable.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SCOPES AND SAFETY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Scopes are a safety mechanism.

Automation without scope
is automation without restraint.

Scopes allow Ashby to:
- limit blast radius
- apply conservative defaults
- require confirmation where needed
- restrict automation to safe contexts

If scope cannot be resolved confidently,
Ashby must pause or ask for clarification.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ABSOLUTE CONSTRAINTS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

No action may occur outside a scope.
No state may exist outside a scope.
No memory may be recalled outside a scope.

Any design that introduces
unscoped action, state, or memory
violates Ashby’s world model.

Scopes are not optional.

They are the foundation upon which
all reasoning, action, and truth are built.

END OF 7.1
