ASHBY CODEX — 7.2
TYPES OF SCOPES (PHYSICAL, LOGICAL, ORGANIZATIONAL)

Scopes in Ashby are not uniform.

They represent different kinds of boundaries that exist in the real world.
Understanding these distinctions is required to reason safely,
apply authority correctly,
and scale the system without flattening reality.

Ashby recognizes multiple scope archetypes.
These archetypes describe shape, not limits.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PHYSICAL SCOPES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Physical scopes represent bounded regions of the physical world.

Examples include:
- a room
- a floor
- a building
- a property
- a zone within a property

Physical scopes define:
- where devices exist
- where sensors report from
- where actions have physical consequences

A light exists in a physical scope.
A thermostat exists in a physical scope.
A camera exists in a physical scope.

Physical scope boundaries prevent:
- actions from leaking across spaces
- automation from affecting unintended areas
- ambiguous targeting

A command such as “turn off the lights”
is meaningless without physical scope resolution.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
LOGICAL SCOPES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Logical scopes represent conceptual groupings
that do not map directly to physical space.

Examples include:
- a device group
- a functional role
- a system capability
- a virtual collection

Logical scopes allow Ashby to reason about:
- related capabilities
- shared behavior
- abstract targets

A logical scope may span:
- multiple rooms
- multiple buildings
- multiple physical scopes

Logical scopes never override physical safety.
They operate within physical constraints.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ORGANIZATIONAL SCOPES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Organizational scopes represent authority and ownership.

Examples include:
- a household
- a business unit
- a tenant
- a customer account
- an operational boundary

Organizational scopes define:
- who may issue commands
- what automations are permitted
- what data may be retained
- what actions require confirmation

Organizational scope boundaries are critical
in multi-user and multi-tenant systems.

No action may cross organizational scopes
without explicit authorization.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SCOPE COMPOSITION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Scopes are composable.

A physical scope may exist inside
an organizational scope.

A logical scope may reference
multiple physical scopes.

This composition allows Ashby to model reality
without collapsing it into a single dimension.

Composition is explicit.
Implicit scope inheritance is forbidden.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ABSOLUTE CONSTRAINTS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

No scope type may:
- bypass another scope’s authority
- override safety constraints
- collapse boundaries for convenience

If a design requires flattening scope types,
the design is invalid.

Scopes model reality as it is,
not as it is convenient to automate.

END OF 7.2
