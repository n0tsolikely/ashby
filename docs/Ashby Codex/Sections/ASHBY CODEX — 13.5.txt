ASHBY CODEX — 13.5
NO-OP HANDLING

NO-OP is the lawful decision to not execute an action because the requested
change is already satisfied by Ashby’s current belief at acceptable evidence quality.

NO-OP is not laziness.
NO-OP is not refusal.
NO-OP is not a silent optimization.

NO-OP is a truth-sensitive claim:
“The world already matches the requested state well enough that no action is required.”

Because NO-OP is a claim about reality, it is subject to Action-Gated Truth Law.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
WHY NO-OP EXISTS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

NO-OP exists to prevent three common failures:

1) COMMAND SPAM
Systems that re-fire commands “to be safe” become noisy, unreliable, and opaque.
Repeated writes without evidence do not create truth.
They create chaos.

2) FALSE ACTION CLAIMS
Systems that say “done” while doing nothing are lying.
Systems that do something while claiming they did something else are also lying.

3) UNNECESSARY RISK
Every execution attempt carries risk:
- timeouts
- partial failures
- unintended side effects
- vendor instability
- blast radius expansion

If no action is needed, Ashby must not touch the external world.

NO-OP is how Ashby remains disciplined under success.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
NO-OP IS A PLANNING OUTCOME, NOT AN EXECUTION OUTCOME
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

NO-OP is produced by planning.

NO-OP means:
- the engine decided not to produce an external ActionRequest that changes reality

NO-OP does not mean:
- a command was sent
- an action was verified
- the system “checked” anything unless a refresh occurred

If Ashby chooses NO-OP without performing a refresh, it must speak from belief,
not from observation.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
WHEN NO-OP IS PERMITTED
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

NO-OP is permitted only when all of the following are true:

1) SCOPE IS RESOLVED
NO-OP cannot exist outside a scope.

2) TARGETS ARE RESOLVED
NO-OP cannot be issued against ambiguous targets.
If target resolution is ambiguous, Ashby must clarify.

3) RELEVANT BELIEF EXISTS WITH ACCEPTABLE CONFIDENCE
The attribute(s) relevant to the request must have confidence that meets policy.

Minimum law:
- If the relevant belief is UNKNOWN, NO-OP is forbidden.

Policy may further restrict:
- whether LAST_KNOWN is sufficient for NO-OP
- whether VERIFIED is required
- how strict freshness must be

4) BELIEF IS WITHIN FRESHNESS REQUIREMENTS
NO-OP must respect staleness and decay.

If the belief is stale beyond policy windows:
- NO-OP is forbidden without refresh
- Ashby must either refresh or act with explicit uncertainty posture

NO-OP is a claim of sufficiency.
Sufficiency requires evidence quality.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
NO-OP UNDER MIXED REALITY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A single user request may involve multiple targets.

Some may already match.
Some may require change.
Some may be unknown.

Rules:
- NO-OP is evaluated per target and per attribute.
- Ashby must not treat “most targets match” as permission to NO-OP globally.
- Targets that require change must be acted on if policy permits.
- Targets that match may be reported as “already set” under lawful language posture.

When targets are mixed:
- planning produces an action plan for only the targets that require change
- NO-OP handling applies only to the subset already satisfied

This prevents a common lie:
“I did it,” when the system skipped half the request.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
LANGUAGE RULES FOR NO-OP
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

NO-OP language must state:
- that no change was needed
- what state is believed
- the evidence posture behind that belief

NO-OP does not have a single fixed sentence.
It inherits its truth posture from the belief used to justify it.

If belief is VERIFIED and fresh:
- Ashby may speak declaratively:
  - “Nothing to change — it’s already at 60%.”
  - “It’s already off.”

If belief is LAST_KNOWN and policy permits last-known NO-OP:
- Ashby must hedge explicitly:
  - “Last known, it’s already at 60%. Want me to check?”
  - “As far as I know, it’s already off.”

If belief is UNKNOWN or stale:
- NO-OP is forbidden
- Ashby must not claim “already”
- Ashby must offer refresh or proceed with an action attempt under lawful posture

NO-OP language must never imply execution:
- “Done” is forbidden unless an action was executed and VERIFIED.
- “I set it” is forbidden if nothing was executed.
- “I checked” is forbidden unless a refresh actually occurred.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
LOGGING AND TRACEABILITY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

NO-OP decisions must be traceable.

Rules:
- the decision must be logged with correlation identifiers
- the belief attributes used to justify NO-OP must be recorded (value, confidence, timestamps)
- the policy threshold that permitted NO-OP must be recorded or derivable

NO-OP is a claim.
Claims must be auditable.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
HOUSESTATE BEHAVIOR UNDER NO-OP
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

NO-OP must not upgrade belief.

Rules:
- NO-OP does not create evidence
- NO-OP must not change confidence upward
- NO-OP must not rewrite timestamps as if verified now

If a refresh is performed as part of NO-OP evaluation:
- only the refresh evidence may upgrade belief
- the NO-OP decision remains separate from the evidence acquisition

NO-OP preserves correctness by doing nothing.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
EXAMPLES (REPRESENTATIVE ONLY)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Example A — Verified and fresh
User: “Set the bedroom lights to 60%.”
Belief: VERIFIED at 60% within freshness window.
Response:
- “Nothing to change — they’re already at 60%.”

Example B — Last-known permitted by policy
Belief: LAST_KNOWN at 60%.
Response:
- “Last known, they’re already at 60%. Want me to check them?”

Example C — Unknown forbids NO-OP
Belief: UNKNOWN.
Response:
- “I’m not sure what they’re at right now. Want me to sync them, or should I set them to 60%?”

These examples are illustrative only.
They do not define a fixed inventory of devices, zones, or policies.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ABSOLUTE CONSTRAINTS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

NO-OP handling must:
- be scope-bound
- require resolved targets
- require acceptable confidence and freshness
- preserve mixed reality per target
- speak within the belief posture used to justify the decision
- remain auditable

NO-OP handling must never:
- claim execution when no execution occurred
- upgrade certainty without new evidence
- ignore staleness to preserve convenience
- collapse partial satisfaction into a blanket claim

NO-OP is discipline under correctness.
